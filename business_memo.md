# Business Memo

**To:** Product, Marketing & Customer Support Leadership  
**From:** Data Analytics Team  
**Subject:** Key findings before launching any retention campaign  
**Date:** September 30, 2025

---

## Executive Summary

An analysis of 2,400 customers across order history, support tickets, web activity, and loyalty data reveals that **47% of our customer base is at risk of churning in the next 60 days.** This is not a minor retention problem — nearly half the customer universe may not make another purchase by end of November 2025.

Before spending any campaign budget, the following findings demand attention.

---

## Finding 1: Recency is the strongest churn signal we have

Customers who last ordered **more than 90 days ago churn at an 83% rate**. Customers who ordered within the last 30 days churn at only 12%. This 7× difference means that any retention effort must be prioritised toward customers who have gone quiet — not toward active buyers who do not need intervention.

**Implication:** Do not run a flat discount campaign across all customers. Focus spend on the ~30% of customers whose last order was 60-90+ days ago.

---

## Finding 2: Over half our customers are not enrolled in the loyalty programme

57.8% of customers (1,386 out of 2,400) have no loyalty tier. While non-enrolled customers churn at a slightly higher rate (48.3%) than enrolled ones (45.1%), the difference is smaller than expected. This suggests the loyalty programme alone is not a strong retention lever — or that it is not being promoted effectively at the right time.

**Implication:** Investigate whether customers are offered loyalty enrolment at checkout or post-first-purchase. The programme's impact appears weak for currently enrolled customers, which may indicate the incentives are not compelling enough.

---

## Finding 3: Zero web/app sessions in the last 30 days is a reliable early warning

Customers with zero sessions in September 2025 churn at **66.3%**. Customers with 6+ sessions churn at under 15%. This means our web analytics already give us a near real-time churn signal — before the 60-day window even closes.

**Implication:** The CRM or product team should set up an automated trigger: if a known customer has had zero sessions for 14+ consecutive days, they should immediately enter a re-engagement workflow.

---

## Finding 4: Heavy discount buyers are more likely to churn when the discount stops

Customers whose average discount was above 50% across their order history show elevated churn rates. This suggests a segment of our buyer base is **discount-sensitive rather than brand-loyal** — they buy when the offer is good and leave when it is not.

**Implication:** Before offering these customers another discount, ask whether it will build loyalty or just buy a single transaction. For this segment, consider switching from discount-based campaigns to value-based content (personalised recommendations, skin-care routines, loyalty point rewards) that does not train the customer to wait for a deal.

---

## Finding 5: Support ticket volume alone is not a strong churn predictor

Customers who raised exactly one ticket showed the highest churn rate (50%), while customers with three or more tickets had the lowest churn rate (35%).

This suggests that ticket volume alone does not explain churn. Customers who repeatedly interact with support may remain engaged with the brand, while customers who leave without seeking help may silently churn.

**Implication:** Support teams should focus on ticket sentiment and issue type rather than ticket count alone. Negative experiences such as refund delays, damaged items, or product reactions may be more useful indicators of churn risk.

---

## What to Investigate Before Campaign Launch

1. **Confirm the priority audience:** Customers with recency > 60 days AND zero sessions in the last 30 days are the highest-risk group. Size this segment first and confirm campaign economics before scaling.

2. **Audit the loyalty programme:** Why is loyalty tier not reducing churn more significantly? Are enrolled customers getting meaningful value? Is the programme visible at the right touchpoints?

3. **Review discount strategy:** Identify what share of revenue is coming from heavily discounted orders. A 47% churn rate combined with heavy discount reliance is a margin and loyalty problem simultaneously.

4. **Close the support loop:** Build a report showing how many customers with negative support experiences received any follow-up outreach. Anecdotally, this number is likely very low.

5. **Fix data gaps before scaling personalisation:** 1,386 customers with no loyalty tier and 401 with no skin type means personalisation campaigns will be blunt for a large portion of the base. Enriching these profiles at next purchase should be a quick CRM win.

---

## Recommended Next Step

Run a segmented retention pilot on the top 200 highest-risk customers (high recency + low sessions + at least one negative support ticket) before deciding on a full campaign budget. Measure 30-day reactivation rate against a hold-out group. This will give a defensible ROI estimate and avoid wasting budget on customers who would have purchased anyway.

---

*Analysis based on 2,400 customer records, 8,137 pre-snapshot orders, 1,921 support tickets, and 30-day web activity data as of 2025-09-30.*
